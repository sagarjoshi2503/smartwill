Who can be a witness? Under the Indian Succession Act, 1925, a Will needs at least two witnesses. Any adult (18 or older) of sound mind who is capable of signing their own name can be a witness — they don't need to know what's in the Will, and they don't need to be related to you. Each witness must see you sign the Will (or you must directly tell them you've signed it), and then sign it themselves in your presence. It's best to avoid using someone who also inherits under the Will as a witness — for Christians and Parsis this can actually cancel that person's inheritance, while for Hindus, Sikhs, Buddhists and Jains it doesn't, though an independent witness is always safer. Family members can technically witness, but independent, impartial witnesses are recommended to avoid any later dispute. Your executor (the person managing your estate) is allowed to be a witness.

Financial Assets

I bequeath all my financial assets — including Bank Accounts, FDs, RDs, PPF, Life Insurance, Stocks, Mutual Funds, Crypto, Digital Wallets, NPS, Bonds, AIF, SIF, and PMS — entirely to the nominees registered in those financial instruments.

Why we recommend this: it's highly advisable to pass on financial assets by way of nomination rather than by listing individual accounts. This single clause ensures every financial asset goes to whoever is currently registered as your nominee on that account. Because account numbers, folio numbers, and balances change frequently, tying the clause to your nominations — instead of specific account details — means you don't need to alter your Will every time a financial detail changes. We simply recommend keeping your nominations up to date across all your accounts and policies.
